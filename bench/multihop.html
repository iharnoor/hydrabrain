The question your notes app can't answer

You saved two hundred things this month. The moment you ask how any of them connect, your search bar goes quiet. That gap is the whole reason a "second brain" still does not feel like one.

By Harnoor · 2026-06-24 · companion to "I caught my own benchmark lying"

Search bars are good at one thing: finding the note that contains the words you typed. The questions that would actually make a pile of saved content valuable are almost never about words. They are about how things connect.

Here is the question I kept wanting to ask my own saved stuff, and could not:

"Which of the people whose posts I saved this month also got mentioned in that thread about the new fund?"

search: returns the thread, and every note with the word "fund." None of them is the answer. The answer is a person, and that person's name is not in your query at all.

graph: start at the thread, follow "mentions" to the people, intersect with the people you saved. The answer falls out of the connections, not the keywords.

That is a multi-hop question. The answer is not in any single note. It lives in the path between notes, and you have to take more than one step to reach it.

01Why the search bar goes quiet

Both kinds of search you have used fail this question for the same underlying reason. Keyword search matches the literal words, so it can only find notes that already contain your query. Vector search (the thing powering most "chat with your notes" features) matches meaning, so it finds notes that are about the same topic as your query. Both are similarity. Neither is connection.

The answer to a multi-hop question is, by definition, not similar to the question. The person you are looking for is not "about" funds. They are linked to the fund through someone else's sentence. Similarity search walks right past them, because nothing about them looks like what you asked.

People assume a reranker fixes this. It does not. A reranker reorders the candidates retrieval already pulled. If the right note was never pulled into the candidate set, no amount of reordering invents it. The missing answer was never on the table.

Similarity finds the note that looks like your question. Connection finds the note that answers it. Those are different notes.

02What "hops" actually means

A hop is one step along a relationship. Most retrieval is implicitly one hop, and one hop is easy:

"who works at company-00"→works_at→person-00

That is a single edge. Even plain search often stumbles into the answer, because the note about person-00 probably says "company-00" right in it. The trouble starts when the answer is two hops away and nothing in the question names the destination:

"which fund backed the company person-00 works at"
person-00→works_at→company-00→backed_by→fund-a

To answer that, a system has to resolve person-00, walk to their company, then walk again to that company's investor. The note holding the answer (fund-a) may never mention person-00 at all. There is nothing for similarity to grab. You need a system that stored the relationships as relationships, and can follow them.

02The graph is the thing that can follow a link

A graph-native memory does not just store your notes as a pile of text. As it ingests, it pulls the relationships out of the prose and stores them as typed edges: works_at, backed_by, mentions, founded. Now a multi-hop question becomes a short walk: resolve the starting entity, follow the edges, return what you land on. The answer comes from the structure, so it does not matter that the destination note shares no words with your question.

This is the part I wanted to measure, because it is the part that decides whether a second brain can actually answer the interesting questions. So I benchmarked it: a graph-native store (HydraDB) against a strong open-source memory engine, on a set of one-hop and two-hop relational questions over the same corpus.

03What the multi-hop numbers say

Relational recall@5 · opponent handed a perfect, hand-built graph
Question typeHydraDBopponent
1-hop ("who works at X")92.3%100.0%
2-hop ("which fund backed the company X works at")86.0%63.8%

The shape here is the real lesson, and it is honest in both directions. On one hop, the opponent wins: when the answer is a single edge away and the graph is hand-built to perfection, deterministic traversal is flawless and hard to beat. But on two hops, the gap opens wide, 86.0% to 63.8%, because chaining relationships is where retrieval systems quietly fall apart, and it is exactly the regime the interesting questions live in.

The honesty tax

This is a small synthetic corpus (50 entities, 149 queries), the opponent was handed a perfect graph to remove any excuse, and the hosted side is non-deterministic so the numbers drift a few points per run. Read them as a signal about multi-hop, not a final scoreboard. The full story, including where I almost fooled myself, is in the companion post.

04Why this is the whole point of a second brain

The dream I am chasing is simple to say: dump in everything I consume, the saves and the videos and the articles and the half-thoughts, then ask it real questions and get real answers. The reason that dream keeps disappointing people is not ingestion. It is retrieval. A second brain that can only find notes that look like your query is just a search box with extra steps. The questions that would make it feel like a brain ("what connects these two things I saved months apart") are all multi-hop, and multi-hop is exactly what the search box cannot do.

So the unglamorous infrastructure question, can your memory follow a link, turns out to be the whole product. Get it right and the saved pile becomes answerable. Get it wrong and you have a very smart way to retrieve the note you already remembered saving.

→See it for yourself

The benchmark is open and runs in one command. It builds the graph, engages the relational traversal, and will refuse to print a number if the opponent's graph was not built correctly:

# one- and two-hop relational retrieval, opponent handed a perfect graph
python3 -m bench.relational_v2 --seed-edges --report
