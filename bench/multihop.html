The question your notes app can't answer

You saved two hundred things this month. The moment you ask how any of them connect, your search bar goes quiet. That gap is the whole reason a "second brain" still does not feel like one.

By Harnoor · 2026-06-24 · companion to "I caught my own benchmark lying"

Search bars find the note with the words you typed. The questions that make a pile of saved content useful are almost never about words. They are about how things connect, and that is a different problem most memory systems quietly fail.

The question I kept wanting to ask my own saves, and could not:

"Which of the people whose posts I saved this month also got mentioned in that thread about the new fund?"

search: returns the thread and every note with the word "fund." The answer is a person whose name is not in your query at all.

graph: start at the thread, follow "mentions" to the people, intersect with the people you saved. The answer falls out of the connections.

That is a multi-hop question. The answer is not in any one note. It is in the path between them, and you have to take more than one step to reach it.

01Why search goes quiet

Keyword search matches words. Vector search, the engine behind most "chat with your notes" features, matches meaning. Both are similarity. The answer to a multi-hop question is not similar to the question: the person you want is not "about" funds, they are linked to one through someone else's sentence. Similarity walks right past them.

And a reranker does not save you. It reorders what retrieval already pulled. If the answer was never pulled in, reordering cannot invent it.

Similarity finds the note that looks like your question. Connection finds the note that answers it. Those are different notes.

02Hops

A hop is one step along a relationship. One hop is easy, because the answer note usually contains the words:

"who works at company-00"->works_at->person-00

Two hops is where it breaks, because nothing in the question names the destination:

"which fund backed the company person-00 works at"
person-00->works_at->company-00->backed_by->fund-a

The note holding the answer may never mention person-00. There is nothing for similarity to grab. You need a system that stored the relationships as relationships and can walk them.

03A graph walks the link

A graph-native store pulls relationships out of your prose as it ingests and keeps them as typed edges: works_at, backed_by, mentions. A multi-hop question becomes a short walk: resolve the start, follow the edges, return what you land on. It stops mattering that the destination shares no words with your query.

So I measured it: a graph-native store (HydraDB) against a strong open-source memory engine, on one- and two-hop questions over the same corpus.

04The numbers

Relational recall@5 - opponent handed a perfect, hand-built graph
QuestionHydraDBopponent
1-hop92.3%100.0%
2-hop86.0%63.8%

On one hop the opponent wins, 100 to 92: with a perfect hand-built graph, deterministic traversal is hard to beat. On two hops the gap blows open, 86.0 to 63.8. Chaining relationships is where retrieval falls apart, and it is exactly where the interesting questions live.

Honest, fast

Small synthetic corpus (50 entities, 149 queries), opponent handed a perfect graph, hosted service so numbers drift a few points per run. A signal about multi-hop, not a final scoreboard. The fuller story, including where I nearly fooled myself, is in the companion post.

05Why this is the whole game

The dream is simple: dump in everything you consume, then ask it real questions and get real answers. What kills that dream is not ingestion, it is retrieval. A second brain that only finds notes that look like your query is a search box with extra steps. The questions that make it feel like a brain, like "what connects these two things I saved months apart," are all multi-hop, and multi-hop is exactly what the search box cannot do.

So the boring question, can your memory follow a link, is the whole product. Get it right and the pile becomes answerable. Get it wrong and you have built a very smart way to find the note you already remembered.

→Run it

One command. Builds the graph, turns on traversal, refuses to score if the graph was not built right:

# one- and two-hop relational retrieval
python3 -m bench.relational_v2 --seed-edges --report
