I caught my own benchmark lying about HydraDB

I forked an open-source agent-memory engine, rebuilt its retrieval layer on HydraDB, and ran a head-to-head. The first numbers were a blowout. They were also a lie - and the lie was mine.

By Harnoor 2026-06-23 HydraDB engineering

I rebuilt an open-source agent-memory engine's retrieval layer on HydraDB, benchmarked it against the real thing (gbrain), and watched HydraDB win everything. Then I found out why: I had been benchmarking gbrain with its main feature switched off.

So I rebuilt the harness to be fair, handed gbrain every advantage, and ran it again. HydraDB still wins where it counts. Here is the whole thing, losses included.

01The blowout was a lie

gbrain's headline feature is a typed-edge graph: it pulls relationships out of prose and lets you traverse them. My harness had it broken three ways, each one quietly against gbrain:

  • BUG 1 - no graph. The default schema pack shipped no edge-inference rules, so gbrain built zero edges. I was scoring a graph engine with no graph.
  • BUG 2 - wrong edges. Once extraction ran, "person-00 works at company-00" came back typed founded. Wrong type, wrong answers.
  • BUG 3 - graph off at query time. gbrain's traversal only fires with relational:true, which I had not set. The graph sat unused.

Reasonable defaults, my job to get right. A benchmark that measures a competitor with its best feature dark is not a benchmark. It is a press release with a chart.

02Rebuilt fair

  • Same prose into both engines.
  • An integrity gate that aborts if gbrain's edges are missing or mis-typed. You cannot re-run the original lie.
  • gbrain's relational arm on.
  • Then the killer: I handed gbrain a 100%-correct, hand-seeded graph using its own method. Best-case gbrain, no extraction excuse left.

gbrain wins clean 1-hop lookups, 100 to 92. One edge away with a perfect graph, its traversal is flawless. But the questions worth asking are rarely one edge away. On multi-hop ("which fund backed the company person-00 works at") HydraDB holds 86.0% to gbrain's 63.8%, even with gbrain's graph hand-built to perfection. That is the gap that matters, and it is HydraDB's.

And that is gbrain at its best. Make both engines extract from raw prose, the way they would in production, and gbrain falls to ~50% recall (its regex extractor recovers only ~44% of the relationships) while HydraDB holds ~88%. Feed both the same documents and HydraDB builds the better graph.

A benchmark you can't rerun is marketing.

04The part that isn't close

Retrieval quality is an argument. The architecture is not.

Dense vectors, BM25, fusion, a reranker, query expansion, a graph arm: six stages, ~29x the code, four services to keep alive. HydraDB folds all of it behind one recall(). Comparable quality, a fraction of the machinery.

05At scale

On LongMemEval-S (~48 distractor sessions per question), HydraDB more than doubled the QA accuracy of a BM25 baseline, 38.1% to 16.7%, on tied evidence recall (97.6%). The one caveat I will not bury: that baseline is BM25, not gbrain. I have not run gbrain at this scale yet, so it says HydraDB turns evidence into right answers far better than lexical search, and nothing about gbrain.

06What is true

  • Multi-hop: HydraDB wins, even against a perfect gbrain graph.
  • Extraction from prose: HydraDB wins, by a lot.
  • Simplicity: one call, one service, ~29x less code.
  • 1-hop: gbrain wins. Single-edge lookups on a clean local graph are its sweet spot.

Caveats, once: small synthetic corpus (50 entities, 149 queries), and HydraDB is hosted, so it is non-deterministic and drifts a few points per run. Signal, not scripture.

07Don't trust this benchmark either

If my own benchmark could be rigged in my favor without me noticing, so can anyone's. The author picks the corpus, the opponent, the metric, the config, and when to stop checking. Every one is a place to hide a win, and the author is the last to see it. The checklist that would have caught me:

  1. Is the opponent real, or a strawman with its best feature removed?
  2. Is that feature actually on? Mine was not.
  3. Did the inputs land? Count them, and abort if not.
  4. Did you give the opponent its best config? Hand it a perfect setup and see if you still win.
  5. Is the metric named honestly? A mislabeled metric is a lie with a chart.
  6. Deterministic? If not, say so and quote ranges.
  7. Can a stranger rerun it in one command? If not, it is a screenshot, not evidence.
  8. Does it survive you arguing the other side?

Reproduce it

One command. It builds the graph, turns on traversal, and refuses to print a number if gbrain's graph is not real:

# fair relational benchmark, gbrain handed a perfect graph
python3 -m bench.relational_v2 --seed-edges --report

Do not trust it. Run it. Try to break it.

HydraDB is a hosted, graph-native agent-memory store - hydradb.com. gbrain is open-source agent memory by garrytan - github.com/garrytan/gbrain. All entities in the benchmark corpus are synthetic placeholders. This is a fair technical comparison, not a dunk: gbrain is a serious, well-built system, and the integrity of the result depends on being generous to it.
